3.2 Introduction to dormancy

Tree dormancy is a state of reduced activity that occurs when environmental conditions become unfavorable, particularly during winter. This state serves as a survival strategy to protect trees from extreme temperatures, water shortages, and other stress factors. During dormancy, trees slow down or halt their growth processes to conserve energy and prevent damage.

3.4 Experimental and statistical determination of the chilling and forcing periods

Dormancy presents two phases during which temperatures have opposite effects on flowering. High chill accumulation during endodormancy advances flowering dates, but similarly cool temperatures during ecodormancy may cause flowering delays. The challenge is to distinguish between these two phases, even though the tree buds appear to be in the same developmental stage throughout. 3.5 Phenology record and BBCH scale

Phenology is the study of periodic events in biological life cycles and how these are influenced by seasonal and interannual variations in climate.

This module will involve working with phenology data sets, primarily focusing on a specific stage, usually budbreak, even though trees pass through various developmental stages during the year. These stages are typically identified by numerical codes. The following video Phenology in temperate fruit trees will cover what these numbers signify. It’s essential to understand these codes to link them to visual signs, like trees covered in blossoms, trees with yellowing leaves, or trees bearing ripe, sweet fruits.
